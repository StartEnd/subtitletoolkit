Use SRT to TXT conversion when you need the words from a subtitle file, not the timing data.

## Quick answer

Open the [SRT to TXT Converter](/tools/srt-to-txt/), paste or upload the `.srt` file, and export the plain text result.

The converter removes SRT cue numbers and timestamp lines, then keeps the subtitle text in reading order.

## When this is useful

Convert SRT to TXT when:

- you need a transcript draft from captions
- a translator only needs the spoken text
- you want notes or quotes without timestamps
- you are cleaning subtitle text before rewriting it

If you still need timings for playback, keep the original SRT file and treat TXT as a separate working copy.

## Step-by-step workflow

1. Keep a backup of the original `.srt` file.
2. Open the [SRT to TXT Converter](/tools/srt-to-txt/).
3. Upload the SRT file or paste the subtitle text.
4. Check that cue numbers and timestamp lines are gone.
5. Review paragraph breaks and repeated lines.
6. Download the `.txt` output for editing, review, or translation.

## What gets removed

SRT files usually contain:

- cue numbers such as `1`, `2`, and `3`
- timestamps such as `00:00:01,000 --> 00:00:03,500`
- blank lines between cues
- subtitle text

TXT output keeps the readable subtitle text and removes the structure used by video players.

## Common mistakes

### Replacing the subtitle source with TXT

TXT is not a subtitle format. It cannot be uploaded as a timed caption file unless another tool retimes it later.

### Expecting perfect paragraphs

Subtitle line breaks are written for screen timing, not article-style reading. After conversion, do a quick pass for paragraph flow.

### Converting a broken SRT file first

If timestamps are malformed, validate or clean the SRT before extracting text. Use the [SRT Validator](/tools/srt-validator/) if the file looks suspicious.
