If you are uploading subtitle files to YouTube, `SRT` is usually the easiest default choice.

YouTube can handle more than one caption format, but most creators and editors get the least friction from SRT. It is easy to inspect, easy to export from transcription tools, and easy to send to a client or teammate before upload.

## Why SRT is the safest default

- It is simple and widely recognized.
- Many editing and transcription tools export it directly.
- Clients and collaborators usually understand it.

SRT also keeps the review process simple. Each cue has a number, a timestamp range, and text. If something is wrong, a reviewer can point to a cue number or paste the block into a message without needing a special editor.

## When VTT still matters

VTT matters more when subtitles are part of a web playback workflow outside YouTube, such as:

- HTML5 video players
- custom video pages
- browser-based caption previews

If you are moving between YouTube delivery and web playback, [SRT vs VTT](/guides/srt-vs-vtt/) is the right comparison page to keep nearby.

For YouTube itself, VTT is rarely necessary unless it is already your source format. If your captions came from a website or web player as `.vtt`, convert them to SRT before handing them to someone who expects the simpler upload format.

## A practical workflow

Use this rule:

1. Keep `SRT` for handoff and upload workflows.
2. Convert to `VTT` when captions need to be embedded or previewed on the web.

If your source file is still styled `ASS`, use [How to convert ASS to SRT for YouTube uploads](/guides/how-to-convert-ass-to-srt-for-youtube-uploads/) first.

## Pre-upload checklist

- Open the file and confirm the first cue starts at the right moment.
- Check that timestamps use SRT comma milliseconds, such as `00:00:01,000`.
- Remove styling or positioning markup that YouTube will not preserve.
- Review non-English characters after export so encoding problems do not reach the upload step.
- Test one middle cue and one final cue to catch drift before publishing.

## If your file is in the wrong format

- Use [SRT to VTT Converter](/tools/srt-to-vtt/) for web playback needs.
- Use [VTT to SRT Converter](/tools/vtt-to-srt/) when a platform or teammate expects SRT.

If the text looks garbled after export, fix encoding before uploading. The [Subtitle Encoding Fixer](/tools/subtitle-encoding-fixer/) can convert legacy text encodings to UTF-8, which is safer for modern caption workflows.
