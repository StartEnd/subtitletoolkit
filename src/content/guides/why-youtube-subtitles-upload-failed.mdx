YouTube subtitle upload failures usually come from file structure, not the words in the captions.

## Quick answer

Use a clean SRT file for most YouTube uploads. If your source is VTT or ASS, convert it with the [YouTube Subtitle Converter](/tools/youtube-subtitle-converter/) first.

Then validate the SRT structure before uploading again.

## Common causes

YouTube may reject or misread subtitles when:

- timestamps are malformed
- SRT milliseconds use dots instead of commas
- cue numbers are missing or duplicated
- the file contains unsupported ASS styling
- the file was saved with broken encoding
- the extension says `.srt`, but the content is VTT or another format

## Step-by-step workflow

1. Keep the original subtitle source file.
2. Convert VTT or ASS to YouTube-ready SRT.
3. Validate the SRT file before upload.
4. Fix encoding if accents or non-English text looks wrong.
5. Upload the cleaned SRT to YouTube Studio.
6. Preview captions in the YouTube player before publishing.

## What to upload

For a simple upload workflow, SRT is the safest format because it keeps:

- cue numbers
- comma-based timestamps
- readable caption text
- no player-specific styling

If your captions need visual styling, uploaded YouTube captions are usually the wrong place for that styling.

## Common mistakes

### Uploading ASS directly

ASS files can contain styles, positioning, and effects that do not map cleanly to YouTube captions. Convert to SRT for upload.

### Renaming VTT to SRT

Changing the extension does not convert timestamps or remove the `WEBVTT` header.

### Ignoring encoding

If characters look broken after upload, fix the subtitle encoding and upload a fresh UTF-8 copy.
